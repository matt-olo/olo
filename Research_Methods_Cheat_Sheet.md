## Chapter 1: Why Study Research Methods

### Core Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Knowledge** | Information believed to be true and reliable |
| **Social Science** | Research on society & human relationships (CJ, criminology, sociology — NOT natural science) |
| **Research Methods** | Systematic processes/steps to conduct research (establishing data-gathering steps, analyzing data, reaching conclusions — NOT "asking a research question") |
| **Data** | Evidence collected to answer a research question |
| **Science** | Knowledge from observable, falsifiable info gathered systematically |
| **Sample** | Subset of a population (e.g., men 25–35 in private sector) |
| **Definition** | Precise meaning of a concept in a study |
| **Ethics** | Norms of moral behavior (not necessarily feelings or laws) |

### Assessing Knowledge

- Confident knowledge comes from **peer-reviewed journals**
- Non-critical assessment = accepting without considering **HOW** knowledge was created
- To assess findings → examine **measurements, sample size, definitions**
- Larger samples = more generalizable
- Access original research + methodology to assess validity
- Research methods has **practical applications** (not just academic)

### Non-Scientific Knowledge Sources

| Source | Key Facts |
|--------|-----------|
| **Traditions/customs/norms** | Passed person to person; subjective; non-research based (e.g., American flag) |
| **Personal experience** | Powerful but NOT reproducible, NOT objective, NOT falsifiable |
| **Authoritative sources** | Professors, clergy, parents (NOT self) |
| **Intuition** | Feelings, senses, gut instincts |

Scientific knowledge ≠ trusted people, observations, intuition, or social media.

### FBI UCR

- Uses **administrative** data sources
- Does **NOT** include unreported crimes

### Stages of Research (ORDER)

> **RQ → Lit Review → Research Design → Collect Data → Analyze → Findings/Conclusions/Policy Implications**

| Stage | Key Points |
|-------|-----------|
| **Research Question** | Guides research, increases knowledge. Opinions ("Do you believe...") ≠ research questions. After RQ → next = lit review |
| **Literature Review** | Summarizes/synthesizes, identifies gaps/limitations, justifies study. Does NOT "guide research" |
| **Research Design** | Precise steps to answer question; includes operationalization. If prior studies exist → add to them |
| **Collect Data** | Interviews, focus groups, surveys, document analysis, quasi-experiments. Resources/affordability affect method choice |
| **Analyze** | Choose technique that BEST ANSWERS the question |
| **Document Methodology** | Allows critical assessment + replication (occurs during design, NOT lit review) |

- Research does NOT begin with data collection
- Purpose of collecting data ≠ identifying gaps
- Research methods = recipe/how-to guide
- Research = guided by answering a specific question

### Key Studies & Ethics

**Tuskegee:** African American men; NOT told they had syphilis; told "bad blood"; medical incentives; families got burial stipends. **Buxtun** = whistleblower (leaked to journalists).

**Nuremberg Code** (prompted by Holocaust):
- Voluntary informed consent
- Positive results for society not achievable otherwise
- Based on prior knowledge (animal experiments)
- Avoid suffering; proportional risk
- Adequate facilities
- Staff fully trained + scientifically qualified
- Subjects free to quit; staff must stop if dangerous
- **Limitation = no enforcement** (self-governance = ineffective)

**Stanford Prison:** Student recognized unethical → Zimbardo stopped.
**Milgram** = human subjects. **Sheridan & King** = NO human subjects.

### Belmont Report

**3 PRINCIPLES:**

| Principle | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|
| **Respect for Persons** | Autonomy; diminished autonomy = entitled to protection |
| **Beneficence** | Do no harm; maximize benefits/minimize harms |
| **Justice** | Fair treatment + fair selection (NOT "do no harm") |

Applies to **human subjects ONLY** (not animals).

**3 REQUIREMENTS:** Informed consent (voluntary + sufficient info) · Assessment of risk/benefits · Selection of subjects.
"Respect for persons" = **principle**, NOT requirement.

### IRB

- Reviews/approves/monitors human research in US (does NOT *create* research)
- Required for federally funded research
- Compared to IRS (necessary)
- Exception = normal educational practices in traditional settings
- History → humans need guidelines/oversight

## Chapter 2: Identifying a Topic, Purpose, & Research Question

### Wallace's Wheel of Science (ORDER, clockwise)

> **Theory → Hypotheses → Research Design → Observation → Analysis/Findings → Empirical Generalizations → back to Theory**

- Science = recursive/never-ending loop
- Easiest to start with **theory**
- Theory = set of interrelated propositions/assumptions/definitions (= criminological theory)
- Hypotheses = statements about expected relationships between variables
- Research design = choosing methodology

### Why Identify Topic/Purpose/RQ

- Empirically based research answers testable questions
- "Compiling/reordering current knowledge" alone ≠ research process
- Real research = guided by RQ + data collection/analysis + creates NEW knowledge
- If research had one purpose = increase empirical knowledge/understanding

### Topic Sources

Published research, **data** (most productive), theory, RFPs, personal experience, reading, viewing, listening, professors, internet.

- Data forms = words, illustrations, actions, numbers (NOT questions)
- Codebooks = every variable + how measured (hundreds/thousands)
- Good topic = drives curiosity, fun, exciting

| Researcher | Topic | Source |
|-----------|-------|--------|
| Brunson | Youth/police | Reading published literature |
| Dodge | Prostitution stings | Personal experience |
| Santos | Hot spots | Personal experience |
| Melde | Gangs | — |

### Four Purposes of Research

| Purpose | Questions It Answers | Examples |
|---------|---------------------|----------|
| **Exploratory** | "What/How/Where is it?" Best when little/nothing known | Brunson, Dodge |
| **Descriptive** | "What are characteristics?" Detailed understanding. NOT for when nothing known | Cuevas |
| **Explanatory** | "Why? What causes/predicts/influences?" | Zaykowski (victim services), Cuevas |
| **Evaluation** | Systematic program assessment using objective criteria. "How improve program?" = evaluation NOT descriptive | Santos |

Overlaps: Cuevas = explanatory + descriptive. Santos = evaluation + experimental.

### Constructing a Research Question (ORDER)

> **Pick topic → Pick purpose → Narrow topic → Construct → Evaluate → Refine (final step)**

- Must contain a **verb** (examine, determine, investigate, describe, identify — NOT "acquire")
- Narrowing methods: consult literature, Google, discuss
- **Evaluate:** Question? Feasible? Interesting? Increases knowledge? Too broad? Too narrow? (NOT "too short")
- Takes ~1.5× longer than expected. Feasibility IS important.
- Cuevas's RQs: rates by gender; risk over time; cultural factors over time (NOT "socioeconomic factors")

### Ethics (Applied from Belmont)

- Justice ≠ "do no harm" (that's beneficence)
- **Milgram:** Lacked beneficence → psychological + emotional harm

### Federal Policy for Protection of Human Subjects

| Subpart | Covers |
|---------|--------|
| **A (Common Rule)** | Fundamental procedures + informed consent + IRB framework. Research = systematic investigation → generalizable knowledge. Human subject = living individual (cadavers exempt) |
| **B** | Pregnant women, neonates, fetuses (NOT cadavers, NOT ex-prisoners) |
| **C** | Prisoners (Kligman experimented without consent) |
| **D** | Children |
| **E** | IRB registration requirements |

Vulnerable population subparts = **B, C, D** only.

**4 Categories of Prisoner Research:**
1. Causes/effects of incarceration & criminal behavior
2. Prisons as institutions
3. Conditions affecting prisoners (disease, victimization, substance abuse)
4. Policies/programs improving prisoner well-being

### IRB Composition & Reviews

- Diverse in demographics + expertise + institutional affiliations
- Must include unaffiliated **community member** (not scientist/participant/educator)
- **3 review types:** Exempt (existing data, unidentifiable subjects) · Expedited · Full board
- Expedited ≠ existing unidentifiable data (that's **exempt**)

### Vulnerable Populations (HHS/NCVS)

Pregnant women · children · prisoners · elderly · minorities · mentally ill. **NOT veterans. NOT ex-prisoners.** Chisolm & Farfel = children.

### Children & Consent

- Need **assent** + parental permission (one parent usually sufficient; both unless one dead/incompetent/unavailable)
- Assent **CAN** be waived (can't understand + direct benefit)
- Child can assent if understands + likely benefits
- Anyone can **withdraw consent at any time**
- NIH offers human subjects training
- IRB must NOT be all same-institution members

## Chapter 3: Conducting a Literature Review

### Purpose

Tell the story of what's known. Demonstrate understanding by: **survey, summarize, synthesize**. Identifies major themes, agreements/disagreements, limitations, gaps (= future research directions). Situates proposed research. Shows how new research creates new knowledge. Can "sell"/market proposed research. Rarely adequately taught. Done BEFORE new research. IS an important part of research.

### Literature Review Roadmap (ORDER)

> **Develop search terms → Search using terms → Identify initial primary sources → Read abstracts → Summarize important info → Create thematically focused table → Prepare for first draft → Write first draft → Edit, proof, polish**

### Sources

- Best primary sources = **peer-reviewed journal articles** (NOT textbooks)
- 3 primary source types: **empirical, theoretical, literature reviews**
- Government reports = NOT primary source journal articles
- Empirical = most commonly used; based on systematic observations/experimentation/experiences
- Theoretical articles **ARE** peer-reviewed
- Feminist theory = theoretical (NOT empirical)
- Theory = set of ideas explaining something (e.g., offending behavior)

### Predatory Publishers

Fake/illegitimate journals; charge large fees; fictitious/unauthorized boards; fabricated impact factors/locations; NOT peer-reviewed; publish questionable articles.

### Peer Review Process

Editor → anonymous manuscript to 3+ experts → recommend reject/revise/accept → editor decides → informs author. Revise & resubmit = same process. **Accepted as-is = RARE. Rejection on first review = COMMON.** Takes more than a few weeks.

### Searching for Sources

| Concept | Key Facts |
|---------|-----------|
| **First step** | Identify search terms |
| **Best tool** | Library electronic search tools |
| **Boolean operators** | AND, OR, NOT — connect OR exclude terms (not ONLY limiting) |
| **Phrases** | Use quotation marks: "police impersonation" |
| **Terms** | Each quoted separately with operator: "police" AND "impersonation" |
| **Best filters** | Type of source + publication date |
| **Saturation** | Search complete, no new results, same citations recurring |
| **After saturation** | Narrow by reading abstracts (best way) |

- Do NOT read all sources in full
- Abstract alone NOT enough to decide keep/discard
- Contemporary ≠ 3–5 years (it's longer)
- Abstracts = ~150–250 words: purpose, methods, findings, conclusions

### Journal Article Structure (ORDER)

> **Abstract → Introduction → Literature Review → Methods → Findings → Discussion → Conclusion → References**

| Section | Key Facts |
|---------|-----------|
| **Abstract** | Block-justified; concise description of results |
| **Introduction** | First normal body text; goal, importance, how adds to literature |
| **Keywords** | On first page |
| **Methods** | Data source (sample), approach, analytic techniques. "Measures" subsection HERE. NOT "purpose of the goal" |
| **Findings** | Immediately after methods |
| **Discussion** | Puts findings in context; interprets; limitations/gaps; future directions |

- Some articles have standalone theory section
- Purpose/RQ found in: **abstract + introduction + conclusion**

### Citation & Writing Rules

- **Full citation:** Author names + year + title + journal name/volume + page numbers (NOT abstract, NOT keywords)
- **APA** = most common in CJ/criminology
- Best reviews = **thematically** constructed (NOT author-based)
- Two approaches: **descriptive + chronological** (NOT evaluative/explanative)
- Descriptive = shares what's currently known

**MEAL Strategy:** Main point · Evidence · Analysis · Linking
- Does NOT need standard order
- Helps overcome lack of organization/structure
- Verbs for main point: "demonstrates" (NOT "usually," "probably," "results")

**Formatting Rules:**
- Use author's **LAST NAME only**
- NEVER write article title in body (only in references)
- Avoid direct quotes; if quoting → quotation marks + page number
- Never use "I"
- Citations NOT always at beginning of sentence

**Thematic tables** = thematically labeled columns; important for organizing.

### Pitfalls

Not enough time · constructing around authors not themes · lack of organization. MEAL helps overcome organization problems.

### Ethics

- **Plagiarism** = using someone else's work as your own
- **Cloning** = copy/paste verbatim without credit
- **Mosaic plagiarism** = replacing words with synonyms, claiming as own

## Chapter 4: Concepts, Operationalization, Measurement, Variables, Data

### Data Types

| Type | Definition | Key Facts |
|------|-----------|-----------|
| **Quantitative** | Numerical, calculates statistics | Uses deductive reasoning |
| **Qualitative** | Non-numerical (narratives, voice intonation, photos, body language, videos, observations — NOT rates) | Uses inductive reasoning. Helps understand e.g., "fears of being a police officer" |

- CJ/Crim uses **BOTH** (no preference for just quantitative)
- Evaluation research often uses both
- Data = individual pieces of info (can be numeric AND non-numeric)

### Reasoning

| Type | Direction | Order |
|------|-----------|-------|
| **Deductive** | General → Specific (quantitative) | Broad theory → Hypotheses → Patterns → Specific data |
| **Inductive** | Specific → General (qualitative) | Specific data → Patterns/themes → Hypotheses → Broad theory |

### Abstract → Concrete (ORDER)

> **Concepts → Conceptualization → Operationalization → Variables**

| Stage | Definition |
|-------|-----------|
| **Concepts** | Abstractions; intangible; immeasurable ideas (e.g., adolescence). Must be defined before explored |
| **Conceptualization** | Precisely/accurately/comprehensively/clearly defining abstract ideas. NOT simple. Quant = researcher defines. Qual = data dictates definitions |
| **Operationalization** | How to INDIRECTLY measure each concept, guided by conceptual definition. Follows conceptualization. Use multiple (Cuevas = victimization). 3 considerations: nature of measures, how many needed, how data recorded. Qual = respondents measure concepts |
| **Variables** | Labels for measures representing concepts. After operationalization → work with variables |

### Variables

| Type | Role |
|------|------|
| **DV (Dependent)** | Focus/outcome of research |
| **IV (Independent)** | Influences/causes variation in DV |
| **Control** | Type of IV to better understand main IV (NOT treated same as DV) |

- Goal = understand IV's influence on DV
- Roles depend on research question
- Variation = fundamental element of research
- If variation among concepts → relationship exists
- Relationship between variables = relationship between concepts

**Examples:**
- "Educational attainment → recidivism": education = IV, recidivism = DV
- "Gender → education": gender = IV
- "Education → years of experience": experience = DV
- Zaykowski: DV = seeking victim services; IVs = reporting to police, demographics, victim distress

### Measures & Attributes

- **Measures** = tools to gather data representing abstract concepts (identified during operationalization)
- **Attributes** = categories/response categories of data for a measure
- **Mutual exclusivity** = no overlap ("never married" = ME; "single" = NOT ME; "married but separated" = ME)
- **Exhaustiveness** = category for every response → "other, please specify"

### Levels of Measurement (ORDER: less → more info)

| Level | Key Characteristics | Example |
|-------|-------------------|---------|
| **Nominal** | Name only; also called CATEGORICAL | Marital status |
| **Ordinal** | Inherent order; Likert scale; NOT called categorical | Satisfaction level |
| **Interval** | Equal known differences; rank-ordered | Temperature |
| **Ratio** | Meaningful zero; count-data | Age |

- Level of measurement = nature/type of data for a variable
- **Discrete** = whole numbers only
- **Continuous** = any value

### Reliability & Validity

| Type | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Reliability** | Consistent, repeatable results |
| **Test-retest** | Same results over time (NOT different raters) |
| **Inter-rater** | Different raters/observers agree |
| **Face validity** | Design assesses intended concept |
| **Criterion validity** | Corresponds to existing measures |
| **Content validity** | Covers full range of concept |

### Pitfalls

Failure to understand process · operationalizing too low · confusing labels · variables that don't vary. Multiple measures recommended (concepts are complex/multidimensional).
