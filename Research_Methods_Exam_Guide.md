# Research Methods Exam Guide (Ch 5, 6, 7, 8)

## CH 5: SAMPLING

**Key Terms:** Population = entire group | Sample = subset | Census = full enumeration | Elements = individual parts of population | Sampling frame = complete list of population | Inference = conclusion about population from sample | Parameter = population summary | Statistic = sample summary | Generalizability = applying findings to population | Bias = sample excludes certain groups | Saturation = no new data from additional subjects | Margin of error = 1/√n | Replication = repeating research

**Probability Sampling** (every element has known nonzero chance): Simple Random (equal chance) | Systematic (every kth element; watch for periodicity) | Stratified (divide into strata, random sample each) | Cluster (sample groups containing elements) | Multistage (combo)

**Non-Probability** (some have zero chance): Convenience/Haphazard | Purposive/Judgmental | Snowball (referral chain) | Quota (match characteristics)

**Units of Analysis:** Individual | Groups/Organizations | Geographic areas | Social artifacts | Interactions. Unit of observation ≠ unit of analysis always.

**Fallacies:** Ecological = applying group conclusions to individuals | Individualist/Reductionist = applying individual conclusions to groups

**Sample Size:** 1,600 = minimal error | Simple random needs smallest n | Probability needs larger n than non-probability | Sample size needed is NOT dependent on population size

## CH 6: QUALITATIVE DATA

**What:** Non-numeric data (text, observations, symbols, culture, feelings, body language). Uses inductive reasoning (specific→general). Open-ended questions. Non-probability sampling common.

**Approaches:** Interviews (structured/semi/unstructured) | Observation & Fieldwork (ethnography) | Document/Content Analysis | Focus Groups | Triangulation = multiple methods/sources

**Role Conceptions:** Complete observer → Observer as participant → Participant as observer (known to contacts, not group) → Complete participant (hidden identity; most ethical issues)

**Coding (Strauss):** Open coding (initial labels/themes) → Axial coding (relationships between categories) → Selective coding (review all data, compare/contrast themes). Grounded theory = theory built from coded data.

**Pitfalls:** Going native = losing objectivity | Must remain objective, no value judgments | All findings must be reported

**Ethics:** Informed consent | Anonymity/confidentiality | Protect participants AND researchers from harm | Complete participant role = deception = biggest ethical concern

## CH 7: SURVEY RESEARCH

**Forms:** Questionnaires (self-administered: mail, in-person, online) | Interviews (telephone, face-to-face)

**Steps:** Research purpose/question → Sampling plan → Distribution → Data collection → Analysis → Answer question

**Key Terms:** Fielded = survey is live | Response rate = returned/total sent | Survey nonresponse = not returned | Interview bias = interviewer influences responses | Recall bias = inaccurate memory | Respondent burden = time/energy to complete | Survey fraud = signing up just for incentives | Processing = coding responses into data | Skip-and-fill = contingent questions | Notification letters = inform of upcoming survey

**Dillman's Principles:** Simple words | Complete sentences | Fewest words | Precise estimates | Balanced response sets | Unbiased comparisons | Both sides of attitude scale | "Undecided" at END | Avoid double-barreled questions | Avoid loaded questions | Avoid "choose all that apply" (use matrix) | Don't ask for math calculations | Strategies to improve recall (shorter time frames) | Mutually exclusive & exhaustive response categories

**Design/Layout:** One question at a time | List answers vertically (not horizontally) | Simple answer boxes | Dark print questions/light print answers | 12pt+ font | Number consecutively | Minimize matrices | Use 11×17 if >1 page | Pretest before fielding

**Administration (Dillman timeline):** Notification letter → Survey 1 week later → Thank-you postcard 2 weeks after → Final reminder 8 weeks after

**Confidential vs Anonymous:** Confidential = researcher knows identity, keeps it private | Anonymous = researcher CANNOT link data to respondent. Cannot be BOTH.

**Online Platforms:** SurveyMonkey (easy, basic) | Qualtrics (advanced, piping, heat maps) | LimeSurvey (free, open-source, needs programming skills, written in PHP not HTML)

**Ethics:** Encrypt data | Don't carry on personal laptops | Appropriate incentives (not too large = coercive; e.g., candy bars OK) | IRB disclosure | Consider time/place for safety | Require responses to prevent missing data

**Pitfalls:** Can't re-survey easily if mistakes found | Use 8th grade language | Avoid jargon | Pretest is best way to ensure useful data

## CH 8: EXPERIMENTAL RESEARCH

**Purpose:** Establish CAUSATION between variables

**3 Criteria for Causality:** 1) Association (IV & DV move together) 2) Temporal ordering (IV before DV; easiest to establish) 3) Lack of spurious relationships

**Spuriousness:** Appears related but isn't. Caused by confounders (affect both IV & DV) or intervening variables (between IV & DV in time). Confounders ≠ intervening (confounders NOT between IV & DV in time). Correlation ≠ causation.

**True Experiments (Gold Standard):** Criteria: ① experimental + control group ② random assignment ③ researcher manipulates IV (=treatment). Designs: Two-group post-test only | Two-group pre-test-treatment-post-test (most rigorous of basic) | Solomon Four Group (strongest overall: 4 random groups, tests for pre-test effects)

**Controls:** Random assignment (controls confounders) | Matching (pairs on relevant variables)

**Pre-Experimental (weak, exploratory only):** One-shot case (one group, treatment, post-test; weak internal validity) | One-group pre-test-post-test (no comparison group, no randomization) | Static-group comparison (two groups, no randomization, no pre-test). Poor scientific value. No comparison of control vs experimental. Cost-effective to determine if more research warranted.

**Quasi-Experimental (no random assignment):** Non-equivalent control group design (two similar non-random groups; selection bias threat) | Before-and-after design (pre/post, no control for confounders). Used when randomization impossible/impractical/unethical.

**Natural Experiments:** Occur outside labs | Researcher does NOT control administration of treatment | Groups form naturally | Has control + experimental groups | Researcher has control over but not administration of treatment

**Internal Validity** = can conclude treatment caused effect. Threats: Selection bias | Maturation | History (external event during study) | Testing (pre-test affects post-test) | Instrumentation (measurement changes) | Experimental mortality (differential dropout) | Statistical regression (extreme scores → mean). High IV from: randomization + manipulation of IV.

**External Validity** = generalizability to real world. Threats: Reactive effects (novelty of participation changes behavior) | Reactivity threats | Sample size too small. Internal validity ≠ external validity (external does NOT establish causality).

**Pitfalls:** Claiming causality without meeting criteria (most common) | Failing to consider internal validity threats | Small sample → generalizability issues | Randomization should be done by 3rd parties | Matching must be on RELEVANT confounders | Choose most rigorous design possible | Solomon Four Group has fewest validity problems
